What is a Volcano?

A volcano is a crack in the earth's crust through which lava, volcanic ash, and gases can escape. Dissolved gas pressure contributes to volcanic eruptions. Under a volcano, liquid magma containing dissolved gases rises through cracks in the Earth's crust. As the magma rises, the pressure decreases, allowing gases to form bubbles. When magma (lava) reaches the surface, its behaviour is determined by its gas content as well as its chemical composition. As layers of rock and ash build-up from repeated eruptions, volcanoes often form a hill or mountain.

Volcanoes are classified as active, dormant, or extinct:

  • An active volcano is one that is likely to erupt in the near future or is currently erupting. Furthermore, active volcanoes have a history of eruption and are likely to erupt again.

  • A dormant volcano is a volcano that is not currently erupting but can also be classified as "active" as it has the potential to erupt in the future.

  • Extinct volcanoes are unlikely to erupt in the near future.
